## About

I am a postdoctoral researcher in the Air-Sea Interaction Laboratory at Scripps Institution of Oceanography, UC San Diego. My research combines laboratory experiments and data-driven analysis to understand how density stratification shapes fluid flows in the ocean.

I received my PhD from the University of Southern California, where I worked with Prof. Geoffrey Spedding in the Stratified Fluids Laboratory. My dissertation focused on the wakes generated by bluff bodies moving through stratified environments — a problem with direct connections to underwater vehicle hydrodynamics and oceanic turbulence. Using towed experiments in a large stratified water tank and dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), I developed new approaches for identifying and classifying wake regimes from limited flow measurements.

At Scripps, I am extending these methods to air-sea interaction problems,
investigating how the techniques I developed for stratified wake analysis can
inform our understanding of upper-ocean dynamics and atmosphere-ocean coupling.

## Research Theme 1: Stratified Ocean Wakes

### Title
Stratified Ocean Wakes

### Description
When objects move through density-stratified water — as submarines, autonomous underwater vehicles, and marine organisms do in the ocean — their wakes behave fundamentally differently than in uniform fluid. Buoyancy forces suppress vertical motions, reorganize vortex structures, and create distinctive internal wave signatures that can persist far downstream.

My experimental work uses a large tow tank with precisely controlled density stratification to study these wakes systematically. By towing scaled models (including a 6:1 prolate spheroid) through stably stratified water and measuring the resulting flow fields with particle image velocimetry (PIV), I have mapped how wake structure transitions across the Reynolds number (Re) and Froude number (Fr) parameter space.

Key findings include the identification of distinct near-wake regimes that depend on the balance between inertial and buoyancy forces, and the discovery that stratification fundamentally reorganizes the streamwise vortex system in ways not predicted by unstratified wake models.

## Research Theme 2: Data-Driven Flow Identification

### Title
Data-Driven Flow Identification

### Description
Real ocean measurements are sparse — sensors are expensive, and you rarely have the luxury of full-field data. How do you identify what kind of flow you're observing from just a handful of measurement points?

I develop data-driven methods that extract physically meaningful flow structures from limited experimental data. My primary tool is dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), a technique that decomposes unsteady flow fields into spatiotemporal modes, each associated with a single frequency and growth/decay rate. Applied to stratified wake experiments, DMD reveals the dominant oscillatory structures — internal waves, vortex shedding modes, and their interactions — that characterize different wake regimes.

Building on this, I contributed to a sparse regression framework (with collaborators at USC) that can identify the wake regime from as few as a handful of point measurements, by matching against a library of known flow signatures. This approach has practical implications for ocean monitoring, where autonomous platforms need to classify flow environments in real time with minimal instrumentation.

My 2022 paper on this topic was selected as an **Editor's Suggestion** in *Physical Review Fluids*.
